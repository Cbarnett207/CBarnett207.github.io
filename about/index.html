About Me

I am a master's candidate in the Department of Geology at the University of Kansas (KU) where I am advised by Dr. Leigh Stearns, who heads the Glaciology and Remote Sensing Laboratory. Prior to pursing my graduate degree at KU, I obtained my B.Sc. in Marine Physical Sciences and a minor in Earth and Climate Science at the University of Maine. During my time at the University of Maine I worked on a wide range of research projects that were focused on earth observations in both the marine and crosphere settings.

My prior research includes a multi-beam bathymetry survey of the Bagaduce River Estuary system and and various ice-penetrating radar surveys in both Maine and Alaska I am interested in using ice-penetrating radar in conjunction with various other remote sensing techniques to quantify spatial and temporal changes along the base of ice shelves caused by oceanic forcing. My current research focuses on using historic NASA Operation IceBridge radar to investigate how both basal melt rates and basal crevasse morphology have changed over the last decade for ice shelves in both northern Greenland and Antarctica. Quantification of ocean-induced basal melt is critical for understanding the dynamics of controlling outlet glaciers Curious about the specifics of my current and past research projects?
