About Me

I am a master's candidate in the Department of Geology at the University of Kansas (KU) where I am advised by Dr. Leigh Stearns, who heads the Glaciology and Remote Sensing Laboratory. Prior to pursing my graduate degree at KU, I obtained my B.Sc. in Marine Science and a minor in Earth and Climate Science at the University of Maine. During my time at the University of Maine I worked on a wide range of research projects that were focused on using geophysical instrumentation for earth observations in both the marine and cryosphere settings. My prior projects include both a multi-beam sonar bathymetric survey of the Bagaduce River Estuary system in Maine, and multi ice-penetrating radar surveys along the Mathes, Llewellyn, and Tulsequah glaciers on the Juneau Icefield in Alaska.

I am interested in using ice-penetrating radar in conjunction with various other remote sensing techniques to investigate spatial and temporal changes along the base of ice shelves caused by oceanic forcing. My current research focuses on using historic NASA Operation IceBridge radar imagery to investigate how both submarine melt rates and basal crevasse morphology have changed over the last decade for ice shelves in both northern Greenland and Antarctica. Quantification of ocean-induced submarine melt along ice shelves is critical for understanding the dynamics of controlling outlet glaciers and the ice sheet as a whole. In conjunction with submarine melt, basal crevasses act as first-order control on when and where discrete iceberg calving events can occur. Though submarine melting and calving are understood to contribute to mass loss along ice shelves, the relationship and interactions between these two processes is not well understood. Using new methods developed by our lab in conjunction with researchers at the Center for Remote Sensing and Integrated Systems (CReSIS) at KU, my research addresses the rate at which melt occurs along specific glaciers, and how melt impacts the growth of crevasses throughout time.
